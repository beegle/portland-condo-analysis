# Portland's luxury condo market offers deep discounts—and deep risks

**Downtown Portland's luxury condo market is firmly in buyer's market territory heading into 2026, highlighted by the Ritz-Carlton Residences' dramatic 50% price reset after its developer lost the building to foreclosure.** A 2-bedroom, ~1,600 sq ft unit at Portland's marquee new tower can now be had for **$700,000–$1,235,000**—roughly half the original asking prices—while comparable units in established Pearl District buildings trade between **$465,000 and $1,180,000**. Record-high office vacancy (**34–35%** in the CBD), persistent livability concerns, and elevated carrying costs have suppressed demand, but declining violent crime, historically low new construction pipelines, and falling mortgage rates point toward a potential inflection. For a patient buyer with a long time horizon, the value proposition is compelling—but the risks are real and specific.

---

## The Ritz-Carlton Residences tell Portland's luxury story in miniature

The **Ritz-Carlton Residences, Portland** (550 SW 10th Avenue, also known as Block 216) is the defining project—and cautionary tale—of Portland's luxury condo market. Developed by **BPM Real Estate Group** (Walter C. Bowen) at a cost of roughly **$600 million**, the 35-story, LEED Gold tower opened its hotel component in October 2023 and launched residential sales in 2024. It contains **132 condominiums** on floors 21–35, sitting atop a 251-room Ritz-Carlton hotel, 160,000 sq ft of Class-A office space, and ground-floor retail including the Flock food hall.

The problem: only **11 of 132 units sold** by the end of 2025. The developer executed a deed in lieu of foreclosure in summer 2025, transferring the remaining ~121 unsold units to lender **Ready Capital**. In December 2025, **Christie's International Real Estate Evergreen** was appointed as exclusive brokerage, and in January 2026, prices were slashed by approximately **50%** from original listings.

For context, the 11 units that previously sold closed at an average of **$1,053/sq ft**—already a significant discount from the original implied pricing of ~$2,096/sq ft. The post-reset Christie's inventory now lists at **$423–$854/sq ft** for 2-bedroom units, representing extraordinary value for a branded residence. Two privately held units listed by LUXE Forbes Global Properties remain priced far higher ($1,272–$1,919/sq ft), reflecting pre-reset aspirations rather than current market reality.

The amenity package is genuinely world-class: a **19th-floor infinity pool and jacuzzi** with Cascade Mountain views, a full **Ritz-Carlton Spa** with five treatment rooms, a **4,000 sq ft fitness facility**, 24-hour concierge, valet parking, an 8th-floor owners' lounge with golf simulator and wine storage, in-residence dining, and housekeeping services. Unit finishes include 10-foot ceilings, floor-to-ceiling glass, **Wolf/Sub-Zero/Miele** appliances, and Pedini Italian cabinetry. These amenities rival anything in Portland and exceed most Pacific Northwest competitors.

The catch is the carrying cost. **HOA fees average $1,700–$2,400/month** depending on unit size—roughly **3× the downtown Portland average** of $784/month. For a 1,600 sq ft unit, expect approximately **$1,800–$2,000/month**. That translates to **$21,600–$24,000 annually** before property taxes, insurance, or mortgage payments. The HOA covers pool, spa, fitness, concierge, valet, building security, and common area maintenance, but it represents a permanent premium that will affect resale competitiveness.

Located directly across from the Portland Art Museum on the South Park Blocks, 2-bedroom units price at just **$465,000–$575,000** ($335–$417/sq ft) with HOA around **$600–$1,000/month**. Built in 2006, it offers 24/7 concierge, rooftop terrace, and a strong HOA—at roughly one-third the total monthly carrying cost of the Ritz-Carlton.

## A deep buyer's market shaped by structural headwinds

The downtown Portland condo market has been in decline since 2018. **Median central-city condo values have dropped approximately 20%** from their 2018 peak, and the trajectory has not yet reversed. The broader market data paints a consistent picture of weakness.

The condo market is running at roughly **5.6 months of housing supply**—well above the 5-month threshold that defines a buyer's market. Average days on market range from **110 to 145+ days** for luxury units, with some properties accumulating **400+ cumulative days**. In the Pearl District, sellers receive only **95% of asking price** versus 98% citywide, and median sale prices fell roughly 2% month-over-month through mid-2025.

The primary structural headwind is **record-high office vacancy**. Portland's central business district vacancy stands at **34–35%**, among the highest of the 25 largest CBDs nationally. Quarterly office leasing fell to **250,000 sq ft** in 2025—the lowest level on record, below even pandemic-era figures. An estimated **59,000 fewer people** are in Multnomah County during the workday compared to 2019. Major office towers have sold at staggering discounts: "Big Pink" (U.S. Bancorp Tower) traded at an **86% discount** from its prior listing. This hollowed-out daytime population directly affects street-level retail vitality, restaurant traffic, and the overall feeling of urban energy that makes condo living attractive.

However, several indicators suggest the bottom may be forming. **Violent crime dropped 17% in the first half of 2025**, with homicides falling **51%**—the steepest decline among 68 major U.S. cities tracked. Downtown foot traffic has recovered to **86% of pre-pandemic levels**, with summer 2025 recording the busiest pedestrian activity since before COVID. Mortgage rates have declined to approximately **6.0% for a 30-year fixed** (down more than a full percentage point from early 2025), and the **multifamily construction pipeline has collapsed to just 656 units**—the lowest since 2011. New condo development specifically has declined **more than 90% over two decades** in Oregon, meaning future supply constraints could eventually support prices. No new luxury condo towers are under construction or announced.

## HOA fees are the hidden variable that reshapes the value equation

The HOA fee differential across Portland's luxury buildings is enormous and materially affects the true cost of ownership. The Ritz-Carlton's **$2,400/month average** translates to **$28,800 annually**—roughly **$12,000–$24,000 more per year** than every competing building.

Over a 10-year hold, the HOA premium at the Ritz-Carlton versus a building like Vista North Pearl (~$817/month) totals approximately **$190,000** in additional carrying costs. Against the Encore (~$500/month), the differential exceeds **$228,000** over a decade. This means a Ritz-Carlton unit priced $200,000 below a competitor may actually cost more to own over time.

Standard Portland luxury building HOAs ($800–$1,400/month) typically cover building insurance, water, sewer, trash, exterior maintenance, elevator upkeep, common area electricity, and building management. Full-service buildings add 24-hour concierge, fitness facilities, guest suites, and community rooms. The Ritz-Carlton's premium buys hotel-grade amenities (pool, spa, valet, in-residence dining) that no other Portland building matches—but buyers should calculate whether they'll actually use $15,000+ worth of spa treatments and valet service annually.

A critical diligence item for any Portland condo purchase: **review the reserve study carefully**. Multiple real estate professionals warn that some Portland HOAs have collected insufficiently low fees for years, creating latent special assessment risk. For the Ritz-Carlton specifically, a **$7.8 million inclusionary housing fee** was due in December 2025, and its payment status remains uncertain—a significant red flag that any prospective buyer's attorney should investigate.

## Five key concerns a buyer should weigh carefully

**Property taxes and the local tax stack are substantial.** Portland's effective property tax rate runs **1.0–1.2% of market value**, putting annual taxes on an $800,000 condo at roughly **$8,000–$9,600**. But property taxes are only part of the picture. Oregon's income tax tops out at **9.9%**, supplemented by Multnomah County's Preschool for All tax (1.5% on income over $125,000 single / $200,000 joint, rising to 3% at higher thresholds) and Metro's Supportive Housing Services tax (1% above the same thresholds). Portland also levies a **$35/year Arts Tax** per adult. A new Parks Levy passed in November 2025 will increase rates by roughly $0.60 per $1,000 of assessed value starting July 2026. The offsetting benefit: Oregon has **no sales tax**, saving significantly on furnishings and renovations.

**Rental flexibility varies by building and is tightly regulated.** Portland requires short-term rental operators to live in their unit at least **270 days per year** and caps absent-owner rental at 95 days annually. A Type A permit is required for Airbnb-style rentals. Beyond city rules, individual HOA bylaws may prohibit short-term rentals entirely. Long-term rentals (30+ days) are generally permitted in most buildings, but owner-occupancy ratios matter: if too many units are rented, **FHA will not approve loans** for any buyer in the building, significantly narrowing the resale buyer pool.

**Resale liquidity is currently poor.** Downtown Portland ranks **80th out of ~8,100 markets** on Realtor.com's market attractiveness index—near the very bottom nationally. Some buildings show discouraging long-term appreciation: a Benson Tower unit sold in 2008 for $458,700 resold in 2024 for $414,500 (down 10% after 16 years). Several other examples show **10–19% nominal depreciation** over 15+ years. The Ritz-Carlton's experience—11 sales out of 132 units, average $274,000 below list—illustrates the challenge of exiting luxury condo positions in this market.

**Earthquake risk is a real but manageable consideration.** Portland sits near the Cascadia Subduction Zone, and standard condo insurance (HO6 policies, averaging **~$633/year** in Oregon) explicitly excludes earthquake damage. A separate earthquake endorsement costs **$150–$300/year**. New construction like the Ritz-Carlton meets modern seismic codes, but older towers (pre-2000) may have structural vulnerabilities. Sewer backup coverage ($75–$150/year) is also essential given Portland's aging stormwater infrastructure.

**The Ritz-Carlton's ownership situation introduces unique risks.** With only 11 of 132 units owner-occupied, the building has extremely low occupancy. The lender (Ready Capital) now controls the vast majority of units and the HOA voting power. The unpaid $7.8 million inclusionary housing fee creates legal uncertainty. Prospective buyers should engage a real estate attorney to review the HOA's financial statements, reserve study, master insurance policy, and any pending obligations before committing. Oregon's **HB 3746** (effective January 1, 2026) shortened the construction defect statute of repose from 10 to 7 years for new buildings—a reform that encourages development but slightly reduces buyer protection windows.

## Conclusion

Downtown Portland's luxury condo market presents a genuinely rare buying opportunity—and genuinely significant risks. The Ritz-Carlton Residences at **$423–$854/sq ft** (post-reset) represent branded luxury at a fraction of comparable projects nationally, while established Pearl District buildings like the Metropolitan, Casey, and Cosmopolitan offer proven quality at **$400–$665/sq ft** with far more manageable HOA fees. The market is unambiguously buyer-friendly: elevated inventory, 100+ days on market, and sellers accepting well below asking.

The core question is timing and conviction. The structural headwinds—34% office vacancy, depressed foot traffic, negative national perception, and a heavy local tax burden—are real and unlikely to fully resolve within 2–3 years. But the supply pipeline has essentially shut off, violent crime is dropping sharply, and mortgage rates have improved meaningfully. For a buyer who plans to hold **7–10+ years** and genuinely wants to live downtown, the price-per-square-foot values available today may not recur. For a buyer seeking near-term appreciation or investment returns, the math remains challenging. The single most actionable insight: **the HOA fee differential between buildings matters as much as the purchase price**, and any comparison that ignores carrying costs will produce misleading conclusions.
