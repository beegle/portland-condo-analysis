The Ritz-Carlton Residences,

A once-in-a-cycle opportunity to acquire branded luxury at 50–65% below original pricing — but only if you understand the carrying costs, structural risks, and what the numbers really say.

Investment Thesis: Conditional Buy

The Ritz-Carlton Residences offer a genuinely rare entry point into branded luxury at $423–$854/sq ft — roughly half the original asking price and competitive with 15-year-old Pearl District buildings. For a second home buyer with a 7–10 year horizon who values the Ritz lifestyle and Bonvoy integration, this is a compelling acquisition if you negotiate aggressively (Ready Capital is bleeding $254K/month in HOA on 121 unsold units), budget for the $2,100+/month carrying costs, and accept that Portland's downtown recovery is a multi-year bet. The single most important insight: HOA fees matter as much as purchase price. Over 10 years, the Ritz's HOA premium vs. Vista North Pearl exceeds $190,000 — enough to erase a substantial purchase price discount. Run the numbers on total cost of ownership, not just the sticker price.

What Happened

A $600 Million Tower That the Market Rejected

Block 216, Portland's tallest mixed-use tower, opened in late 2023 as Oregon's first Ritz-Carlton. The 35-story tower includes a 251-room hotel, 160,000 sq ft of Class-A office space, and 132 luxury residences on floors 21–35.

The residences launched in 2024 at roughly $2,096/sq ft — pricing that the Portland market emphatically rejected. By end of 2025, only 11 of 132 units had sold, and 8 of those recorded zero days on market (likely pre-sales or insider transfers, not open-market absorption).

In summer 2025, developer BPM Real Estate Group executed a deed-in-lieu of foreclosure, transferring the unsold inventory to lender Ready Capital. In January 2026, Christie's International Real Estate announced a 50–65% price reset across all remaining units.

This reset has fundamentally repriced the entire Portland luxury condo market downward.

Why It Matters for You

As a pied-à-terre buyer ($1.2–1.8M, 7–10 year hold, 8–12 weeks/year personal use), you're entering at what may be a generational low for Portland luxury:

  • ◆ No new luxury condos in the pipeline (90% decline)
  • ◆ Violent crime down 17%, homicides down 51%
  • ◆ Mortgage rates improved to 6.0% (from 6.63%)
  • ◆ World-class amenities at a fraction of coastal pricing

But Consider Carefully

  • ⚠ 34% office vacancy — highest in the nation
  • ⚠ HOA of $2,100+/mo (3x downtown average)
  • ⚠ $7.8M inclusionary fee — status unknown
  • ⚠ Oregon tax stack adds $30–40K/year for high earners
  • ⚠ 121 of 132 units still unsold — liquidity risk
